Introduction to the Genesee River Wilds

A Neglected Resource

The Genesee River is one of the most spectacular rivers in the northeastern USA. The river originates with small tributaries flowing northward from the ridge of a triple continental divide in Potter County, PA. These quickly gather volume on their downhill run toward their confluence in Genesee, PA. After crossing the PA/NY state line, the river's power gradually increases before plunging over three breathtaking falls in Letchworth State Park, NY. At the park's northern end the river exits a massive flood mitigation dam in Mt. Morris. From there it flows more lazily through rolling farmlands toward New York State's third largest city. In Rochester it cascades over another three majestic falls before emptying into the vast viewscape of Lake Ontario. The river's great beauty and its value as one the largest tributaries into a Great Lake justify significant investment in its protection and nurture.

Two events occurred in 1972 that should have aroused even more interest in its conservation. One was the devastating flooding of the Genesee River resulting from Hurricane Agnes. The other was the passage of federal laws now classified under the Clean Water Act. In 1987 these were supplemented by an agreement between the USA and Canada that identified the Rochester Embayment as one of 43 "Areas of Concern" in the Great Lakes. In the subsequent decades heroic efforts did indeed produce some noteworthy achievements in the conservation of the Genesee River.

Forested buffers provide wildlife habitat; shade to cool water for fish; food for acquatic life eaten by fish; a natural water purification system; carbon storage to limit climate change; and buffers against costly flooding, erosion, and sedimentation. Restoring wide forested riparian (streamside) buffers is far less expensive and far more effective in the long run than other efforts to achieve these goals.

Yet by the early 2000s it had become increasingly clear that improvements in the water quality in one part of the Genesee watershed were being undermined by the more rapid pace of opposing developments in others. Repeated studies of the Genesee River's water chemistry revealed only insignificant improvements in phosphorus and sediment loads since a pilot study that began in 1974 (e.g, 1974-78, 1991, 2000, 2002, 2004, 2007). Phosphorus and other agricultural runoff feed algae blooms that endanger Lake Ontario's fishery and the health of shoreline communities. Sediment deposited into the river by erosion imposes a burden on taxpayers by requiring dredging at Mt. Morris Dam and the Rochester harbor.

More recent research has reaffirmed the results of past studies. A study in 2012 concluded that the Genesee River still ranked 32nd among all rivers in the country in pounds of toxic materials discharged into its waters. Studies published by teams from SUNY Brockport in 2013-2014 reached the unsurprising conclusion that the Genesee River ranks as number one source of phosphorus among all tributaries into Lake Ontario from New York State alone. Out of all other tributaries into the lake it is second only to the Niagara River, which drains all the other Great Lakes. This research has also demonstrated that the upper Genesee River south of Letchworth State Park is a significant source of these problems. This is certainly not a new discovery, especially to the river's advocates in the upper Genesee watershed. However, the unprecedented detail of the SUNY Brockport research has brought welcome attention to what they already had been saying for many years.

Investments that had produced measurable successes near the river's mouth in Rochester have never been complemented by comparable investment in the upstream area south of Letchworth State Park. Even federal and state officials with regional responsibilities extending into the rural headwaters area in the south often prefer to devote their attention to parts of the watershed with higher profiles. The proximity between the stunning views in Letchworth and the adjacent portion of the watershed immediately south of the park makes the relative lack of investment in the southern portion especially puzzling. Given the ease with which the beauty, wildlife habitat, and water quality in the rural upper reaches of the Genesee River can be coaxed back when given relief from abuse, the impression of the southern part of the watershed has long been one of profoundly tragic neglect.

Genesee River Wilds

Discussions of these problems by a few locals in the late spring of 2008 led to the formal launch of the Genesee River Wilds initiative later that summer. This was staged in Belvidere, NY, on August 11, 2008. Attendees symbolically affirmed partnerships emerging across state lines in NY and PA.*

The fledgling group's rudimentary watershed plan was called Genesee River Wilds Project: Comprehensive Program when it was completed in Sept. 2008. The plan subsequently appeared without the subtitle in website format, which allowed incremental updates. The most significant was the 61-page PDF of the Triple Divide Trail System Strategic Plan released in Sept. 2010 (with minor updates in March 2011). This detailed a proposed system of watershed conservation, blueways, multi-use trails, and nature parks from Rochester, NY, to Williamsport, PA. This represented a key element of the Genesee River Wilds initiative's foundational concepts and drew upon discussions with officials from both NY and PA that had been going on since the spring of 2008. These plans shaped the charter documents of Genesee River Wilds, Inc., which was registered in New York State in 20?? and granted 501(c)3 status by the IRS in 20??. The Genesee River Wilds Plan of 2016 will promote wider recognition and guide increased collaboration.

Terminology

The Genesee River Wilds Plan identifies its targeted portion of the Genesee River watershed as the "Genesee River Wilds." This refers to the section of the Genesee river basin from the river's sources at the triple continental divide in Potter County, PA, downstream to the current southern boundary of Letchworth State Park in Portageville, NY.

The phrase "Genesee River Wilds" plays multiple roles. It expresses this plan's goal of restoring the historic corridors of forested wilderness along the Genesee River and its major tributaries. Allusion to the excitement of "wild" behavior also epitomizes the plan's strategy. Interest in restoring riverside wilderness is promoted through outdoor recreation. The phrase also serves as a marketing tool, which illustrates the plan's concern with eco-tourism. The Genesee River Wilds target area also directly overlaps the magnificent 12-county region of PA known as the Pennsylvania Wilds. This allows "Genesee River Wilds" to represent the sharing of strategies, energy, and resources across the NY/PA state line in the target area. This partnership has been central to the original plan's approach since the summer of 2008. The revised version of the Genesee River Wilds Plan continues to integrate conservation, recreation, and economic development by promoting the creation of minimally-invasive infrastructure for eco-tourism and other sustainable business on a scale that can only be achieved by collaboration across state lines.

"Upper Genesee River" is also used to refer to the target area because this phrase is more familiar outside the region. The phrase refers to the southernmost sections of the Genesee River because the river flows northward. In this plan the geographical scope of "upper Genesee River" is synonymous with that of "Genesee River Wilds." Both refer only to the part of the basin south of the southern boundary of Letchworth State Park. This contrasts with the common practice of drawing the boundary between the "upper Genesee" and the "lower Genesee" further north at the confluence of the Genesee River and Canaseraga Creek in Mt. Morris, NY. But the Genesee River Wilds Plan is hardly unique in this use of the terminology. Pragmatic considerations often segment out a "middle" section or create a secondary boundary in Portageville. One of the most important recent studies of the "Upper Genesee River Watershed" cited Portageville in this way to limit its own research boundaries to the same portion of the watershed addressed in this plan (Makarewicz, Lewis, Snyder, and Smith 2013 [Vol. 2]). Even NYSDEC and NYSOPRHP reaffirm this tradition in the actual implementation of their own plans because both agencies place their regional administrative boundaries near Portageville. In contrast to more restricted efforts, this plan is not limited by the state line.

Approach

The primary strategy for water conservation advocated in this plan is remarkably simple: Plant trees. More fully, create permanent buffers of riparian (streamside) forests that restore the Genesee River while protecting it from future development along its edges. As the section of this plan devoted to this issue indicates, the plan follows the recommendations of the USDA and other agencies in its goals for the minimum widths of forested riparian buffers. Instead of trying to tame the river with costly artificial structures that inadvertently encourage even more irresponsible behavior, the emphasis on restoring forested riparian buffers in this plan represents what is often called a "natural systems approach." In contrast to the spiraling costs of building more and more rock and concrete structures to address the new problems created by counter-productive behaviors, this plan's approach is more economically sustainable in the long run.

More distinctive to this plan is its approach to the challenging problem of how to get people to apply this otherwise simple strategy. The thesis of the Genesee River Wilds Plan is that creating a massive nature park system that extends the benefits of such a system to an equally wide array of stakeholders offers the necessary regional scale and needed variety of incentives. This is expressed in the plan's broader overall strategy of creating a system of nature parks and green spaces that effectively extends Letchworth State Park to the river's sources in PA one piece at a time.

Models

Previous plans for conservation of the Genesee River have been surprisingly timid about pursuing a model of conservation that stares every planner in the face. Letchworth State Park in NY is arguably the most successful model for conservation of the Genesee River in the entire watershed. The emphasis on forestry in the Genesee River Wilds Plan finds other models in the watershed in the protective forests around Hemlock and Canadice Lake and the patches of state forest lands around small headwaters streams. However, Letchworth best illustrates the excitement that can be generated by integrating conservation with outdoor recreation, education, eco-tourism, and other priorities in this plan. The same strategy has proven successful in many other nature parks, greenways, and other settings that otherwise cannot match the spectacular features of Letchworth. This plan not only applies such an approach, but also capitalizes on the success of Letchworth by directly tying the Genesee River Wilds into Letchworth itself.
