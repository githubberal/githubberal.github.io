USDA recommended sizes (in feet) of forested riparian buffers for selected functions. Image courtesy Robert Tjaden, Glenda Weber, and Maryland Cooperative Extension. Forested buffers provide wildlife habitat; shade to cool water for fish; food for acquatic life eaten by fish; a natural water purification system; carbon storage to limit climate change; and buffers against costly flooding, erosion, and sedimentation. Restoring wide forested riparian (streamside) buffers is far less expensive and far more effective in the long run than other efforts to achieve these goals.


It's all about the trees: The Genesee River as viewed looking eastward through trees on the river's western bank on land donated to Genesee River Wilds, Inc., near Caneadea, NY. The land is next to NYS Rt. 19 at the site known as the "Great Angle." The name of the "Great Angle" is derived from its location at the point in which the Genesee River turns from an overall northwesterly flow to a primarily northeasterly direction. On the hill behind the eastern bank seen in the distance was a Seneca Indian village with a large council house. In the 1700s the Council House was frequented by Chief Shongo and reportedly echoed with the voices of famous Senecas such as Red Jacket and Cornplanter. The trees preserved by Genesee River Wilds at the Great Angle represent a small sample of the plan's goal for restoring historic buffers that once protected the river and its riparian habitat.
